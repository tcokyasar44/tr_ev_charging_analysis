\section*{Abstract}
\vspace{10pt}
\noindent\textbf{Objectives}\\
T\"{u}rkiye is rapidly expanding its electric vehicle (EV) charging network, yet whether the current 9,641 EPDK-licensed stations can sustain cost-competitive intercity travel remains an open question. This paper assesses the readiness of T\"{u}rkiye's charging infrastructure for long-distance personal vehicle trips by jointly scheduling charging stops for millions of EVs and comparing the resulting travel costs against conventional internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles.\\

\noindent\textbf{Methods}\\
We apply a scalable, solver-free optimization framework to the full T\"{u}rkiye intercity highway network constructed from OpenStreetMap data and a synthetic demand of 3 million origin--destination trips. The methodology comprises infeasibility pruning, per-vehicle optimal schedule computation via dynamic programming on a directed acyclic graph, and capacity-aware congestion resolution through an iterative penalty heuristic. Economic outcomes are evaluated through a three-tier analysis spanning operational costs, total cost of ownership, and amortized infrastructure investment.\\

\noindent\textbf{Novelty}\\
This study provides the first countrywide assessment of intercity EV charging readiness for T\"{u}rkiye, combining vehicle-level scheduling optimization with economic evaluation over a network of nearly 10,000 charging stations and 3 million synthetic trips derived from intercity travel statistics.\\

\noindent\textbf{Practical Applications}\\
The analysis identifies geographic gaps in T\"{u}rkiye's charging coverage, quantifies the detour and waiting cost burden for intercity EV travelers, and informs targeted infrastructure investment decisions by the Energy Market Regulatory Authority (EPDK) and the General Directorate of Highways (KGM).
