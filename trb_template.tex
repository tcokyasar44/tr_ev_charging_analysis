\section*{Abstract}
\vspace{10pt}
\noindent\textbf{Objectives}\\
This paper assesses whether T\"{u}rkiye's 9,641 Energy Market Regulatory Authority-licensed (EPDK) charging stations can support cost-competitive intercity electric vehicle (EV) travel compared to internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles.\\

\noindent\textbf{Methods}\\
A directed acyclic graph-based scheduling framework optimizes charging stops for over 2 million intercity EV trips across T\"{u}rkiye's highway network (31,286 nodes, 55,236 links) constructed from OpenStreetMap data. Eight case study scenarios cross four charger power levels (5--350~kW) with two station capacity assumptions (5 and 50 plugs), and a 200-run sensitivity analysis examines EV range, discharge rate, charger power, and state-of-charge bounds. A three-tier economic analysis compares operational costs, total cost of ownership, and infrastructure investment.\\

\noindent\textbf{Findings}\\
All scenarios achieve 100\% trip feasibility under a 10--100\% state-of-charge operating window, but feasibility collapses to 0.3\% under a conservative 20--80\% range. EVs are 23--26\% cheaper than ICE at the operational level across all scenarios, driven by T\"{u}rkiye's favorable fuel-to-electricity price ratio. At the total cost of ownership level, EVs retain a 10--12\% advantage. Infrastructure amortization determines the sign of full-system savings: positive at 5 plugs per station, negative at 50. Discharge rate (energy efficiency) is the dominant sensitivity lever at 12.5 percentage points.\\

\noindent\textbf{Novelty}\\
This is the first countrywide, vehicle-level assessment of intercity EV charging readiness for T\"{u}rkiye or any comparable emerging market.\\

\noindent\textbf{Practical Applications}\\
Results inform EPDK and KGM on charger coverage gaps, station sizing tradeoffs, and the economic case for accelerating EV adoption incentives.
